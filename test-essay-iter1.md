# What I Learned from Failing at My First Startup

I quit my job at a bank in March 2019 to build a tool that helped small retailers manage inventory. The idea was simple: barcode scanning plus cloud sync plus automatic reorder alerts. I had worked in retail IT for four years. I knew the pain points. I had a co-founder who could code. We had twelve thousand dollars in savings between us.

The first three months were the best part. We built the MVP in my apartment, the kind of apartment where the kitchen and living room are the same room and the air conditioner runs on a prayer. My co-founder, Jake, coded eighteen-hour days and I wrote copy that nobody would ever read. We launched to fifty beta users in June. Twenty-three of them signed up. Fourteen actually used it. Three paid.

Those three paying customers were the problem. Not because they were difficult. Because they were the only people using the thing, and every time one of them mentioned a feature request, we built it. Jake added barcode scanning for products without barcodes. Then barcode scanning for handwritten labels. Then barcode scanning for products in languages we did not support. Each request felt reasonable. Each one took a week. After six months, our inventory tool could scan seventeen different barcode formats and sync to four different cloud providers and still could not tell you what was in stock.

I should have said no more. I know this now. But saying no felt like admitting we did not know what we were doing, which we did not, but that is not the same thing as admitting it.

The real breaking point came in November 2019. We had a meeting with a regional grocery chain that wanted to pilot our tool across twelve locations. Jake and I spent three weeks building a custom integration with their existing SAP system. We stayed up until four in the morning the night before the demo. The demo went perfectly. The grocery chain said they would get back to us in two weeks.

They did not get back to us in two weeks. They did not get back to us in two months. I emailed. I called. I left voicemails. The silence was so complete it felt like a character in the story. In March 2020, I found out from a blog post that they had acquired a different inventory startup for two million dollars. The startup had been founded six months after ours and had three employees.

I remember sitting in my apartment reading that blog post and feeling something that was not quite anger and not quite sadness. It was more like recognition. We had been building the wrong thing. Not the wrong product. The wrong company. We built features. They built relationships. We had a better tool. They had a better story.

The grocery chain did not need a tool that could scan seventeen barcode formats. They needed a partner who understood their supply chain and could walk them through the transition. We could have been that partner if we had spent three weeks on a spreadsheet showing them how our tool fit into their existing workflow, instead of three weeks building a SAP integration they never asked for.

After that, things unraveled slowly. Jake got a job at Google in April. I kept working on the product for another three months, mostly because I did not know what else to do. I sent cold emails to two hundred companies. Fourteen replied. Zero converted. I shut it down in August 2020 and went back to banking.

Here is the thing nobody tells you about failing at a startup: the failure itself is not the hard part. The hard part is the eighteen months after, when you are sitting in a cubicle at a bank and you keep thinking about what you would have done differently if you had just said no to that one feature request in August 2019. The one where Jake added barcode scanning for handwritten labels and I did not push back because it felt like progress. It was not progress. It was avoidance.

I still think about that grocery chain sometimes. Not with bitterness. More with curiosity. I wonder what their supply chain actually looked like, and whether our tool could have actually helped, and whether the three of us — me, Jake, and the grocery chain — were ever going to be the right combination. I do not think we were. But I wish we had found out.
