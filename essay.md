# What Working Remote Actually Taught Me

I started working from home in March 2020 like everyone else. My company sent an email on a Tuesday: "Work from home until further notice." I packed my monitor into a cardboard box, took the subway home, and set up on my kitchen table. I thought it would last two weeks.

Five years later I still work from that same table. The kitchen table. Not a standing desk. Not a home office. A table I bought on Craigslist for forty dollars in 2017. My knees hit the underside when I cross my legs. The finish wore off where my wrists rest. I never bought a better one because I kept thinking the real office would open again.

That first year was the hardest. Not because of the work — the work was fine. Because of the boundaries. My apartment was seven hundred square feet and suddenly it was also my office, my meeting room, my lunch break, and my commute. The walk from my bed to my desk was twelve steps. I counted. There was no moment where I left work, no train ride to decompress. I closed my laptop at six and the room was exactly the same.

I got better at it. Everyone did. By 2022 I had routines. I walked the dog before standup. I blocked my calendar for lunch. I stopped answering Slack after seven. But something else was happening that I did not notice until much later. The casual conversations disappeared. The ones you have in the hallway after a meeting, walking back to your desk, where someone says "that went okay" and you say "yeah but did you see Carol's face" and they laugh and you get the real story. Those moments did not survive the transition to Slack.

Here is what nobody told me and what I wish someone had: remote work is great for deep work. It is terrible for weak ties. The people you see every day but do not work directly with — the ones from other teams, other floors, other departments — they vanish. And those are exactly the people who give you context about what is actually happening in the company. The promotion you did not know was open. The reorg that nobody announced yet. The project that is getting canceled next quarter but has not been communicated.

I missed three signals in 2023 because I did not have weak ties. My team was reorganized. I found out from a leaked slide. A colleague I had worked with for four years left the company. I found out from LinkedIn. A new initiative I would have been perfect for was staffed before I knew it existed. Not because anyone was hiding it from me. Because the people who knew about it were not in my Slack DMs.

I have thought a lot about whether I would trade back. Five years of no commute, flexible hours, being home for my kid, cooking lunch in my own kitchen. That is a lot to give up. I do not think I would go back. But I think the companies that figure out the weak-ties problem will win the talent war. Not the ones with the best remote tooling. The ones that build intentional social infrastructure.

I do not know what that looks like yet. Some companies try weekly syncs that are not about work. Some do offsites. Some have Slack channels for non-work stuff. None of it quite replaces the hallway. Maybe nothing does. Maybe we just have to accept that remote work gives us deep focus and takes away casual connection, and that is a trade we choose to make.

I still have the Craigslist table. The finish is gone. My knees still hit the underside. I could buy a better one anytime. I keep not doing it. Maybe because the table reminds me of the two-week stretch I thought this would last. Or maybe because I am still waiting for the office to reopen.
