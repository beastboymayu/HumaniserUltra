# What Nobody Tells You About Working From Home

I thought I was ready for remote work. I had the setup right, a dedicated desk, a good chair, noise-canceling headphones, a monitor that did not flicker. I had read the articles. I knew the advice: set boundaries, take breaks, change out of pajamas. None of it prepared me for the silence.

The office has a hum. You do not notice it until it is gone. There are footsteps, phone conversations happening two cubicles over, a printer warming up, someone microwaving fish at eleven in the morning and everyone pretending not to notice. Working from home has none of that. It has your refrigerator. It has the mailman. It has the existential weight of a Tuesday afternoon when nobody has emailed you in three hours and you start wondering if you still exist.

I spent my first six months working from home convinced I was doing it wrong. I followed every productivity system. I blocked my calendar into thirty-minute increments. I color-coded my tasks. I tried the Pomodoro Technique, which is Italian for "I will check Twitter twenty-two times in the next hour." Nothing stuck not because the systems were bad, but because the problem was not productivity.

The problem was that nobody was watching.

When you work in an office, you have an audience. Someone sees you arrive early. Someone sees you stay late. Someone sees you hunched over a spreadsheet at two in the afternoon looking like you are doing something important. That audience is exhausting, but it is also useful. It provides a rhythm, a structure, a reason to pretend you have your life together. At home, there is no audience. You close your laptop at five-thirty and nobody knows you closed it at three.

Here is what I wish someone had told me: working from home is not about discipline. It is about loneliness. Not the dramatic kind, not the I-haven't-spoken-to-another-human-in-days kind. The small kind. The kind where you realize you have not said a single word out loud since breakfast, and it is now dinner time, and your voice sounds strange when you finally order pizza.

The solution, for me, was not better habits. It was making myself harder to ignore. I joined a coworking space twice a week. Not because I got more work done there I actually get less. But because being around other people working, even strangers, made my own work feel real. I could see that I was not alone in the struggle to care about a spreadsheet at three in the afternoon.

I also stopped trying to optimize my morning routine. The people who wake up at five, meditate, journal, exercise, and start work by seven exist mostly on LinkedIn. I wake up at eight, make coffee, stare at the wall for twenty minutes, and start work around nine. That is not a failure. That is just how I work.

One thing I am still not sure about: whether the loneliness gets better or worse over time. I have been doing this for four years now, and some months I feel like I have figured it out. I have routines. I have systems. I leave the house at least once a day. Then something shifts, a project ends, a friend changes jobs, and suddenly I am back in the silence wondering if I have been doing this wrong the whole time. Maybe that is just how it is.
